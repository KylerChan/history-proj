Key Figures in Military Technology

Throughout the 20th century, the world saw a huge leap in military technology. However, these advancements could not have been possible without these people. For example, without Oppenheimer, we wouldn't have nuclear weapons, and without Turing, modern computing and artificial intelligence would not exist.

J. Robert Oppenheimer (1904-1967)

"Now I am become Death, destroyer of worlds"

Theoretical Physicist • Scientific Director of the Manhattan Project

Robert J. Oppenheimer, also nicknamed the "father of the nuclear bomb", was an American theoretical physicist who served as the director of the Manhattan project. He oversaw the development of the first nuclear weapons. Being born and growing up in New York City (NYC), Oppenheimer obtained a degree in chemistry from Harvard University in 1925 and a doctorate in physics from the University of Göttingen in Germany in 1927. He then studied under Max Born, who was also a theoretical physicist who was instrumental in the development of quantum mechanics. After many years of research at other universities, he became a part of the physics facility at the University Of California Berkeley, where he became a professor in 1936. He developed the Born-Oppenheimer approximation in quantum chemistry, which has had lasting impacts on the understanding of chemical reactions. The Manhattan project, which he overlooked, ushered in the nuclear age and fundamentally altered global politics, warfare, and the potential for Mutually Assured Destruction (MAD). The Trinity test on July 16, 1945, marked Oppenheimer's greatest achievement. Witnessing the first atomic explosion in the New Mexico desert, he famously recalled a line from the Hindu scripture Bhagavad Gita: "Now I am become Death, destroyer of worlds."

Alan Mathison Turing (1912-1954)

"Sometimes it is the people no one expects anything from who do the things that no one can imagine"

Mathematician • Logician • Codebreaker • Computer Scientist

Alan M. Turing, born on June 23, 1912, was an English mathematician, computer scientist, logician, cryptanalyst, philosopher, and theoretical biologist. He was highly influential in the development of theoretical computer science, providing fundamental concepts of algorithms and computation through the Turing machine, which served as a model for general-purpose computers. Turing is widely considered the father of theoretical computer science. Born in London to a civil service family, he demonstrated exceptional mathematical ability from an early age. At Sherborne School, his mathematics teachers recognized his extraordinary talent, though they expressed concern about his unconventional approach to problem-solving.

During World War II, Turing played a crucial role at Bletchley Park, Britain's secret codebreaking center, where he worked on breaking the German Enigma cipher. His contributions to cryptanalysis, including the development of the Bombe machine, helped Allied forces decode thousands of intercepted German messages, significantly shortening the war and saving countless lives. After the war, Turing continued his groundbreaking work in computing, designing one of the first stored-program computers, the Automatic Computing Engine (ACE). He also pioneered the field of artificial intelligence, proposing the famous "Turing Test" as a measure of machine intelligence. His work on morphogenesis (the biological process that causes an organism to develop its shape) laid important foundations for understanding pattern formation in nature. Tragically, Turing's life was cut short when he died in 1954 at age 41, but his revolutionary ideas about computation, artificial intelligence, and mathematical biology continue to shape our modern technological world. Today, every computer, smartphone, and AI system traces its conceptual roots back to Turing's visionary work, making him one of the most influential figures in the history of technology.
