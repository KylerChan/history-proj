Key Figures in Military Technology

Throughout the 20th century, the world saw a huge leap in military technology. However, these advancements could not have been possible without these people. For example, without Oppenheimer, we wouldn't have nuclear weapons, and without Turing, modern computing and artificial intelligence would not exist.

J. Robert Oppenheimer (1904-1967)

"Now I am become Death, destroyer of worlds"

Theoretical Physicist • Scientific Director of the Manhattan Project

Robert J. Oppenheimer, who was known as the “father of the atomic bomb”, who was born on April 22 1904 and met his untimely death on February 18, 1967, also was the director of the Manhattan Project[1]. Born in New York City, he got a degree in chemistry from Harvard in 1925 and a doctorate in physics in 1927 from the University Of Göttingen in Germany, and he studied under Max Born. After long research at other prestigious institutions, he joined the staff at the University Of California, Berkeley, where he was a professor in 1936[2]. He was a key figure in military technology, primarily due to him being the director of the Manhattan Project, which was a research and development program undertaken during World War II to produce the first nuclear weapons [3]. It cost ~$2 billion USD in its totality, hiring ~130,000 people at its peak. Oppenheimer was director of the Los Alamos Laboratory and responsible for the research and design of an atomic bomb[4]. It was safe to say that without him, nuclear weapons would not have existed at all. With the power of the atomic bomb on America’s side, it helped America flip the war in their favour, and had the power to spread chaos and destruction nation-wide. “Now I am become Death, the destroyer of worlds”[5].

Alan Mathison Turing (1912-1954)

"Sometimes it is the people no one expects anything from who do the things that no one can imagine"

Mathematician • Logician • Codebreaker • Computer Scientist

Alan M. Turing, who was born on 23 June 1912, and died on 7 June, 1954, was an English mathematician, computer scientist, logician, cryptanalyst, philosopher and theoretical biologist [1]. He was highly influential in the creation of theoretical computer science, and he provided a form of concepts within algorithm and computation within the Turing machine, which could be considered a model of a general-purpose computer[2]. He was widely considered to be the father of theoretical computer science. Being born in southern England, he graduated from King’s College, Cambridge, and in 1938, he got his doctorate degree in Mathematics[3]. During WWII, he worked for the Government Code and Cypher School at Bletchley Park, Britain’s codebreaking centre that made Ultra. It was an Allied intelligence project that tapped the very highest level of encrypted communications of the German armed forces, as well as those of the Italian and Japanese armed forces, and thus contributed to the Allied victory in World War II[4]. One other factor of his importance was that he played a crucial role in cracking intercepted messages that enabled the Allies to defeat the Axis powers in the Battle of the Atlantic and other battles [5].
